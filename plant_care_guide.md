# Houseplant Care Guide

*Compiled from two conversations: organizing plants by room/light, and a humidifier discussion.*

**Home context:** Mt. Baker, Seattle. Most plants watered weekly via kitchen-sink soak with full drainage. Plant food in the water once a month, May–September. Dry indoor air, with gas fireplace heat in winter and mini-split AC on hot days.

---

## Plant Inventory & Care, by Room

### Hallway — South-facing window (brightest spot)

This is the plant hub with 9 plants. A south window in Seattle is prime real estate for light.

**Plants 1 & 2 — Raven ZZ Plants** *(Zamioculcas zamiifolia 'Raven')* — direct sun, currently watered weekly
New shoots dying is almost certainly overwatering, not a light issue. ZZ plants store water in thick rhizomes and only need watering every 2–3 weeks, even in summer. Weekly soaking keeps the soil too moist and rots tender new growth. Cut back to every 2–3 weeks and let the soil go fully dry between waterings. Direct south sun is a little intense for ZZs (understory plants) — if you see yellowing or bleaching, scoot them back a foot.

**Plant 15 — Snake plant** *(Sansevieria trifasciata, variegated yellow edge)* — direct sun, monthly watering
Watering schedule is perfect. It needs repotting, though — roots growing out the drainage hole mean it's very root-bound, and a single small hole is poor drainage for a succulent-type plant. Go up one pot size with a terracotta pot and well-draining cactus/succulent mix. Direct south sun is fine.

**Plant 16 — Heartleaf philodendron** *(Philodendron hederaceum, trailing)* — direct sun, weekly light watering
Clearly thriving — don't change a thing. Growing like crazy in direct south light with regular watering tells you the conditions are dialed in.

**Plant 17 — Anthurium** *(Anthurium andraeanum, + heartleaf philo)* — bright indirect, corner
Good placement; anthuriums like bright indirect and would scorch in direct sun. Watering by can is fine, but make sure the pot drains — anthuriums are prone to root rot.

**Plant 8 — Dragon tree** *(Dracaena marginata / reflexa)* — bright indirect, off to the side
Good spot, good care. Low-maintenance. Brown tips, if any, usually come from tap-water fluoride — Seattle water is relatively low in fluoride, so you're probably fine.

**Plant 12 — Chinese evergreen (green/cream)** *(Aglaonema)* and **Plant 14 — Silver-blue philodendron** *(Philodendron hastatum / glaucophyllum)* — bright indirect
Both well-placed. No flags.

**Plant 13 — Chinese evergreen (white/silver, large)** *(Aglaonema)* — medium indirect, floor ⚠️
Losing volume and dropping leaves. Sitting on the floor below a south window means much less light than the plants on the sill. Chinese evergreens tolerate low light but don't thrive in it long-term. Elevate it on a plant stand closer to window height. Your cautious watering (bone dry, then medium soak) is smart since these rot easily — light is the bigger issue here.

### Kitchen — North-facing window

North in Seattle means no direct sun ever, just ambient daylight. This limits what will thrive.

**Plant 3 — Red nerve plant** *(Fittonia albivenis)* — bright indirect
Fittonias do well in north windows — forest-floor plants that burn in direct sun. Weekly watering is fine. Not wilting dramatically when it misses a watering is a good sign it's not stressed.

**Plant 4 — Cast iron plant** *(Aspidistra elatior, variegated)* — bright indirect 🚨
Sticky leaves are a red flag — almost certainly a pest, most likely **scale insects** or **mealybugs**. The sticky residue (honeydew) is the waste of sap-sucking insects. Check leaf undersides and stems for small brown bumps (scale) or cottony white clusters (mealybugs). Treat with neem oil or insecticidal soap ASAP and isolate from other plants. The dead leaf tip is likely pest-related.

**Plant 7 — Zebra plant** *(Aphelandra squarrosa, flowering!)* — bright indirect, watered every 3 days ⚠️
Notoriously thirsty and humidity-hungry. Needing water every 3 days in a north window suggests the pot is too small and/or the soil dries out too fast. Repot into a slightly larger pot with moisture-retentive soil (add peat or coco coir) to extend the interval — though you likely won't get it to once a week. Flowering indoors is impressive and rare; after the flowers fade, cut the flower spike back and it should push new growth. North light is fine (no direct sun), but it really wants humidity — group it near other plants or set the pot on a pebble tray.

*Best potting soil for the zebra plant (Amazon):* it wants a light, fluffy, mildly acidic mix (pH 6–6.5) — African violet mix is the go-to.
- **Best overall: Espoma Organic African Violet Mix** (~$8–10 / 4 qt) — peat moss, humus, perlite, yucca extract; balances moisture and aeration; organic.
- **Best budget: Miracle-Gro African Violet Potting Mix** (~$8 / 8 qt) — peat + perlite, slightly acidic, more volume, feeds up to 6 months.
- **DIY:** 50/50 standard potting soil and African violet soil.
- Recommendation: Espoma. When repotting, size up just one pot, and use a pot with multiple drainage holes — that alone should help stretch watering toward every 5–7 days.

### Living Room — South-facing window with UV filter

The UV film reduces light intensity, making this effectively bright indirect for most plants.

**Plant 6 — Ripple peperomia (dark)** *(Peperomia caperata)* — bright indirect
Well-placed. Peperomias like bright indirect and moderate watering. No flags.

**Plants 9 & 18 — White/green nerve plants** *(Fittonia albivenis)* — bright indirect, crispy/browning tips
Crispiness on fittonias is almost always humidity. Dry indoor air (gas fireplace, AC) is tough on them. Mist more often, use a pebble tray, or group with other plants. The light is good here — humidity is the limiting factor.

**Plant 10 — Black rubber plant (large)** *(Ficus elastica 'Burgundy')* — medium indirect, floor ⚠️
Losing half its volume on the floor below the window — same pattern as Chinese evergreen #13. Rubber plants want bright indirect. Elevate it on a stand, or move it right next to the window. Watering every two weeks is reasonable and misting is a nice touch, but it needs more light to recover fullness.

**Plant 19 — Corn plant (tall, ~20 years old!)** *(Dracaena fragrans)* — bright indirect, floor
Twenty years is impressive. Brown spots are common with this species — sensitive to fluoride and minerals in tap water. Use filtered water or let tap water sit out overnight. Every 3–4 weeks is a good interval. Low humidity from gas heat and AC doesn't help — a good candidate to sit near a humidifier in winter.

**Plant 20 — Variegated rubber plant 'Tineke'** *(Ficus elastica 'Tineke')* — bright indirect ⚠️
Variegated plants need more light than solid-green ones (less chlorophyll). The UV filter is cutting the light it needs. Swap it with a hallway plant so it gets the south hallway window without UV film. Lack of new growth is a classic sign of insufficient light for a variegated ficus.

### Living Room — Northwest-facing window (lowest light)

Northwest gets late-afternoon sun in summer but very little the rest of the year.

**Plant 11 — Stromanthe 'Triostar'** *(Stromanthe sanguinea 'Triostar')* — bright indirect
A decent match — stromanthes burn in direct sun and like bright indirect; a couple hours of summer afternoon light is fine. Humidity-sensitive, so watch for crispy edges in winter when the gas fireplace is running.

**Plant 21 — Jade plant** *(Crassula ovata)* — medium indirect ⚠️ **Move this.**
Jades are succulents that want as much bright light as possible. A northwest window is too dim — it'll get leggy and stay dark instead of developing the compact, red-edged look of a happy jade. Move it to the hallway south window for direct sun. Water every 2–3 weeks there.

### North-facing window (kitchen-adjacent newer additions)

**Plant 22 — Purple passion (bolting)** *(Gynura aurantiaca)* — medium indirect, new ⚠️
Wants bright light to keep its vivid purple. In medium indirect north light it'll fade and get leggy. Move closer to the glass on the kitchen windowsill, or to a south window in the hallway/living room. Note: when these flower, the blooms smell terrible and it often signals end of lifecycle — they're typically short-lived (2–3 years) but propagate easily from cuttings.

**Plant 23 — Autograph tree / Hoya** — medium indirect, weekly watering ⚠️
Browning and curling leaves suggest weekly watering is too much for a north window where soil stays moist longer. These are semi-succulent and prefer to dry out between waterings. Try every 2 weeks. North light is also on the low end — it'd prefer a living room or hallway south window.

### New Additions (location TBD)

**Plant 24 — Wandering Dude** *(Tradescantia zebrina)* — bright indirect to indirect, weekly watering
Thrives in bright indirect light but tolerates lower light — color vibrancy (that deep purple underside and silver-green stripe) is strongest with more light. Water when the top inch of soil is dry; it wilts dramatically when thirsty but bounces back quickly. Trim leggy stems to keep it full and bushy. Fast grower, easy to propagate from cuttings in water. Not toxic to humans but mildly irritating to pet skin, so keep away from cats and dogs.

**Plant 25 — African Violet** *(Streptocarpus ionantha)* — bright indirect, weekly light watering ⚠️
Bright indirect is essential — too little light and it stops blooming, too much direct sun scorches the fuzzy leaves. Water from the bottom (set the pot in a saucer of water for 20–30 minutes, then drain) to avoid crown rot; water on the leaves causes brown spots. Room-temperature water only — cold water leaves white rings on the leaves. Fertilize every 2–4 weeks with an African violet fertilizer to sustain blooming. Humidity-sensitive; the dry indoor air here may cause leaf curl in winter.

**Plant 26 — Red Chinese Evergreen** *(Aglaonema, red/pink variety — likely 'Siam Aurora' or similar)* — bright indirect, weekly watering ⚠️
Unlike the green aglaonemas (12, 13) which tolerate lower light, red and pink varieties need brighter conditions to hold their vivid color — medium indirect at minimum, bright indirect preferred. The flower spike forming is normal; let it develop or remove it early to redirect energy to foliage (the blooms aren't ornamental). Water when the top half of the soil is dry; this is one of the easier aglaonemas to care for. Watch the color — if the red fades to plain green, it needs more light.

**Plant 27 — White/Cream Chinese Evergreen** *(Aglaonema, green/white mottled variety)* — medium to bright indirect, light watering
The heavy cream-and-white variegation means less chlorophyll, so it needs more light than the all-green varieties — medium indirect at minimum or the pale patches may brown at the edges. Water conservatively; these rot easily in soggy soil. Let the top 1–2 inches dry out between waterings. No cold drafts — aglaonemas are tropical and sulk below 60°F. The full, bushy form in the photo is a sign it's been happy; maintain consistent light and avoid repotting until roots show through drainage holes.

## Humidity & Humidifiers

**Warm mist vs. room-temp mist — does it matter?** For the plants themselves, no. By the time mist disperses into the room and reaches the leaves, it's just water vapor at room temperature regardless of how it left the machine. Plants only "see" the resulting humidity level, not how it got there.

Where the difference actually shows up is practical:

- **Warm mist** boils water first, so output is essentially sterile (less risk of spreading bacteria/mold, no mineral dust). Downsides: more electricity, a heating element that needs descaling, and you can't run essential oils through it (not that you'd want to near plants).
- **Cool mist** comes in two types, and *this* distinction matters more than temperature:
  - *Ultrasonic* (likely what's in the photo with the visible plume): quiet and efficient but throws "white dust" — fine mineral particles that settle on leaves and surfaces. Relevant here since many of these plants have patterned or fuzzy foliage where residue shows and can clog leaf pores. Running distilled or filtered water basically eliminates it.
  - *Evaporative* uses a fan and wet wick: no white dust, self-regulating (can't over-humidify), but noisier and the wick needs swapping.

**Bottom line when buying:** skip the warm-vs-cool framing. Choose based on dust (distilled water or evaporative if it bothers you), noise tolerance, and how much you'll keep up with cleaning. A dirty humidifier of *any* temperature is the one thing that genuinely hurts plants, since it aerosolizes mold.

**Where to aim it:** The genuine humidity-lovers are the fittonias (nerve plants), the maranta, the stromanthe 'Triostar', the ctenanthe, the Aphelandra zebra plant, and the anthurium. Gas fireplace heat is brutally drying in winter. The ZZ plants, snake plant, jade, and rubber trees won't care either way. A 42% reading is fine, but the humidity-divas are happiest closer to **55–60%** — aim the humidifier toward that crowd.
